Microbial Cell Physiology

Cell Division & Developmental Growth Programs

Mechanisms of cell division

 The highly conserved bacterial cell division protein FtsZ polymerizes to form a dynamic protein structure called the “Z-ring” at midcell. At the Z-ring, the center of an E. coli cell "constricts" to divide into two identical daughter cells and FtsZ has been widely suggested to provide the force for constriction. Using in vivo and in vitro assays, we are investigating proteins that recruit, stabilize and destabilize the Z-ring as well as systems responsible for precise timing and placement of the Z-ring in vivo. We recently found that E. coli FtsA can induce changes in the shape of cell membrane at the location of the FtsZ interaction!!!
~~~~~~
As bacteria evolve antibiotic resistance mechanisms to currently available drugs, the need for new antibiotics grows. The division pathway is essential for bacterial survival and there is great potential for new antibiotics to disrupt protein interactions important for cell division. .

Molecular Machines

Performing mechanical work at the nanometer scale

Reconstituting cell machinery

Molecular pathways are often highly complex with specific mechanisms being governed by multiple protein-protein interactions and protein-lipid interactions, and further regulated by enzymatic activities such as ATP or GTP hydrolysis. We take a reductionist approach and sequentially investigate each biochemical event in vitro. With an ever-expanding library of purified proteins and lipids we employ a combination of classical biochemical, biophysical and high resolution microscopic techniques to investigate specific molecular events. Recently, we have been investigating the polar oscillation of the Min system, direct interactions between FtsZ, FtsA and the lipid bilayer, and multivalent targeting of FtsZ by the ClpXP AAA+ protease.

Protein Remodeling

Disassembly, reactivation and homeostasis

Chaperones and proteolysis

Molecular chaperone proteins maintain intracellular protein homeostasis in all living organisms. When these homeostatic mechanisms are disrupted, cells are less able to cope with environmental stress and maintain normal physiology and function. We study how proteins misfold and aggregate and how molecular chaperones promote reactivation from aggregates and partner with proteases for degradation in vivo and in vitro.
~~~~~
Protein homeostasis in bacteria, yeast & neurons.
Key cellular events under investigation in multiple organisms that are proteolytically regulated include cell division, dormancy, inheritance and clearance of amyloids and other aggregates.
